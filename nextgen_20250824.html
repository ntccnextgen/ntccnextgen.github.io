Bible study: Equipped for Good Works: Serving Others with Love

Theme Scripture for today
Galatians 5:13–14


“13For you were called to freedom, brothers. Only do not use your freedom as an opportunity for the flesh, but through love serve one another. 14For the whole law is fulfilled in one word: “You shall love your neighbor as yourself.”“

  • Freedom in Christ
  • Serve One Another
  • Love as the Motive

Bible study: Equipped for Good Works: Serving Others with Love (continued)

  • Freedom in Christ

    • Paul reminds the Galatians that salvation in Christ means they are no longer slaves to the law or sin.
      • But freedom is not a license for selfishness. True freedom is the ability to live as God intended—serving others in love.

  • Illustration: Think of a driver’s license. Freedom to drive doesn’t mean recklessly speeding—it means using the privilege responsibly to reach good destinations and keep others safe.

Bible study: Equipped for Good Works: Serving Others with Love (continued)

  • Serve One Another
    • The Greek word for “serve” (douleuō) literally means “to become a slave.” Paul flips the concept: we are freed from sin’s slavery but now willingly serve others in humility.
    • Christian service is not about recognition, but about meeting needs with Christlike love.
  • Illustration:
    • Jesus washing the disciples’ feet (John 13:1–17).
    • He showed that leadership in God’s kingdom comes through humble service.

Bible study: Equipped for Good Works: Serving Others with Love (continued)

  • Love as the Motive
    • Paul connects service with the commandment: “Love your neighbor as yourself” (Leviticus 19:18).
    • Service without love can look like charity, but it doesn’t reflect Christ’s heart. Love transforms service into worship.
  • Illustration: A meal cooked without seasoning can fill the stomach but not delight the taste. Service without love may meet a need, but it doesn’t nourish the soul.

Bible study: Equipped for Good Works: Serving Others with Love Discussion

  • Love & Service
    1. Why is love essential when we serve?
    2. Can service without love still honor God? Why or why not?
    3. What’s the difference between serving out of obligation versus serving out of love?
  • Motivation
    1. How does freedom in Christ empower us to serve others?
    2. What do you think Paul means when he says the “entire law” is fulfilled by loving your neighbor?
  • Application
    1. Who in your life can you serve this week in a small, practical way?
    2. What gets in the way of serving others with love (e.g., busyness, pride, fear)? How can we overcome those barriers?
    3. How can serving with love impact friendships, families, or even people outside the church?

Response

  • Experience of serving other for praises or accolades.

  • How to change to serve with love?

Sharing: What is Love in Bible?

  • 1 Eros (ἔρως) – Romantic Love • Meaning: Passionate or romantic love, often associated with desire. • In the Bible: While the exact Greek word eros does not appear in the New Testament, the concept is present in the Song of Solomon (celebrating marital intimacy). • Healthy View: God created eros for marriage, pointing to intimacy and covenant.

  • 2 Philia (φιλία) – Friendship Love • Meaning: Affectionate love between friends or siblings; mutual care. • In the Bible: Used in John 15:13–15, when Jesus says, “Greater love (philia) has no one than this: to lay down one’s life for one’s friends.” • Healthy View: This is brotherly/sisterly love, the foundation for fellowship and community (think of Philadelphia — “city of brotherly love”).

  • 3  Agapē (ἀγάπη) – Unconditional, Divine Love
  •   Meaning: Selfless, sacrificial love that seeks the good of others.
  •   In the Bible: The highest form of love, used in John 3:16 (“For God so loved [agapē] the world that He gave His one and only Son”).
  •   Healthy View: This is God’s love for us, and the love He calls us to show to others. It’s not based on feelings but on willful, sacrificial action.

🎵 Lyric highlights

  • “Change my heart o God” is written by Eddie Espinosa in 1982, this worship classic emerged not in a studio, but from a moment of heartfelt prayer.
  • The inspiration struck while Espinosa was stuck in traffic near Disneyland, praying for God to transform his heart. He began to sing the lyrics spontaneously, as if receiving divine dictation. At the next light, he grabbed scrap paper to jot down the words, preserving the song that would soon resonate worldwide.
  • The lyrics “Change my heart, O God… may I be like You” express raw humility and sincere surrender. It’s a proactive, personal prayer to become more like Jesus.
    • Teaching the story behind the song—written in a traffic jam—helps youth see that God inspires worship even in everyday moments.

🎵 Lyric highlights

  • God’s Unchanging Nature: The hymn emphasizes that God “changest not” and has “no shadow of turning”

  • Daily Mercies: The famous refrain declares “Morning by morning new mercies I see”

  • God’s Provision: Celebrates how “All I have needed thy hand hath provided”

  • Creation’s Witness: References the seasons and natural world as testimony to God’s faithfulness

  • Eternal Promises: Highlights “Pardon for sin and a peace that endureth” and “Strength for today and bright hope for tomorrow”

  • Author Thomas Chisholm wrote this hymp in 1923, which was inspired by simple daily experiences of God’s faithfulness rather than dramatic life events.

Bible study: Crucified – Old Self Declared Dead

Theme Scripture:
Galatians 2:20 (NKJV)


“I have been crucified with Christ; it is no longer I who live, but Christ lives in me; and the life which I now live in the flesh I live by faith in the Son of God, who loved me and gave Himself for me.”

🔍 Related Scriptures

  • Galatians 5:24 – “Those who belong to Christ Jesus have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires.”

  • Romans 6:4–6 – “Our old self was crucified… that we might walk in newness of life.”

  • Colossians 3:3–4 – “You have died, and your life is hidden with Christ in God.”

  • 2 Corinthians 5:15 – “…so that those who live might no longer live for themselves but for him who died and was raised.”

📖 Bible Study: Galatians 2:20

Exposition in four points:

  • Paradox of death & life – Though “dead” to the old self, we live spiritually through Christ
  • Not I, but Christ – Our new identity is Christ Himself living in us—He shapes our thoughts and actions.
  • Living by faith – Our daily life is sustained not by law or self-effort, but by trusting Jesus.
  • Christ’s sacrificial love – His death was personal and intentional: “who loved me and gave himself for me”.

💡 Application Points

  1. Let go of past labels — physically discard labels that no longer define you.
  2. Welcome Christ’s life — begin your day saying, “Christ lives in me.”
  3. Practice daily faith — before tasks, pause and choose to trust Christ.
  4. Live out gratitude — look for ways today to express thanks for His sacrifice.

🗣️ Discussion Questions

  • What “old labels” or habits feel like they define you?
  • What does your life look like after being “crucified” with Christ?
  • How can trusting Christ shape decisions this week?
  • How does knowing He died specifically for you change your identity?

🔹 Week 2 – Christ Lives in Me

Key Scripture:
Romans 8:10 (ESV)
> “But if Christ is in you, although the body is dead because of sin, the Spirit is life because of righteousness.”

🧠 Discussion Questions

  • What does it mean that Christ lives in me rather than just I live like Christ?
  • How does His presence shape your decisions, friendships, and emotions?
  • Can you share a moment when you sensed Jesus actively guiding or comforting you?

🎯 Applications

  • Mirror Affirmation: Write “Christ lives in me” on a mirror; declare it aloud each morning.
  • Soul Check-Ins: Pause 2–3 times daily to ask: “Am I living from Christ in me or from my own strength?”
  • Prayer Prompt: “Jesus, help me sense Your life within me today.”

🔹 Week 3 – Living by Faith in the Son of God

Key Scripture:
Galatians 3:11 (ESV)
> “Now it is evident that no one is justified before God by the law, for “The righteous shall live by faith.”

🧠 Discussion Questions

  • What’s the difference between living by faith vs. trying harder on your own?
  • Which areas of your life need stronger trust in Christ?
  • Why is faith in the Son of God different than any other kind of faith?

🎯 Applications

  • Faith Journal: Write down one area needing faith daily (“trust in exams,” “friendship conflict”) and note where God shows up.
  • Accountability Pair: Share your faith goal for the week; check in midweek.
  • Prayer Prompt: “God, help me to live today by faith in Your Son, not by my own strength.”

🔹 Week D – Loved & Given for Me

Key Scripture:
John 15:13 (ESV)
> “Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends.”

Galatians 2:20 reminder
> “…who loved me and gave himself for me.”

🧠 Discussion Questions

  • What difference does it make that Jesus personally gave Himself for you?
  • Have you experienced sacrificial love that reminded you of Christ’s?
  • How can you show sacrificial love to someone this week?

🎯 Applications

  • Gratitude Prayer: Spend quiet time thanking Jesus for His love; share one personal gratitude each.
  • Love in Action: Commit to one sacrificial act (helping someone, encouraging, volunteering).
  • Reflection Prompt: “How does knowing I’m personally loved empower me to love others?”

🙏 Prayer & Reflection

  • Quiet moment: Sit with Galatians 2:20, asking God to reveal areas still tied to your old self.
  • Group prayer: “Christ, thank You… You’ve replaced my old self with Your life. Help me walk by faith today.”
